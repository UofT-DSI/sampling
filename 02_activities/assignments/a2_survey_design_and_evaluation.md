## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

- Sample type: The survey uses a probability sample (a stratified design with one respondent per selected household) as part of a cross-sectional sample survey.
- Sample size: The documentation states that ‚Äúa field sample of approximately 50,000 units was used‚Äù and ‚Äúabout 40,000 invitation letters to the electronic questionnaire were sent ‚Ä¶ A completion of 24,000 questionnaires was expected.‚Äù However, the exact achieved sample size (i.e., number of respondents) is less clearly specified in the publicly available summary. The PUMF documentation lists 16,149 observations in the microdata file version: ‚Äú955 variables, 16,149 observations‚Äù. 
- Target population: Persons aged 15 years and older living in private households in the ten provinces of Canada (i.e., excluding Yukon/NWT/Nunavut) and excluding full-time (six months or more) residents of institutions.
- Sampling frame: The frame is described as: groups of telephone numbers (landline or cell) associated with a given address (or a single telephone number if the number-address link couldn‚Äôt be established) plus the dwelling frame from Statistics Canada/Address Register. The frame therefore attempts to cover households with telephone numbers (including cell-only) and households generally via the address register.
- Survey mode(s): The 2018 cycle introduced an online (Internet) questionnaire option for the first time. In addition, computer-assisted telephone interviewing (CATI) was used. Proxy interviews were not allowed.
- Timeline: 
    - Reference period: the past 12 months preceding the interview date.
    - Data collection period: from September 4, 2018 to December 28, 2018. 
    - Data released: January 26, 2021
- Response rate: The publicly available summary and user-guide do not clearly publish a single overall response rate figure. The documentation mentions ‚Äúabout 24,000 questionnaires was expected‚Äù but does not clearly state achieved response rate in the summary. 
- Weights: Weights were derived from the design weights based on the probability of selection, then adjusted for non-response and post-stratified (calibrated) to align with known population totals from Statistics Canada‚Äôs demographic estimates (by province, age, and gender). Users of the Public Use Microdata File (PUMF) must apply these weights when producing population-level estimates to avoid biased results.
- Data processing: The survey used built-in edits and flow checks during data collection. The CATI system automatically enforced valid response ranges and ensured proper skip patterns. Additional consistency edits were conducted at Statistics Canada‚Äôs head office to identify and correct logical inconsistencies (for example, mismatched birth date and age, or missing demographic fields). Data from different modes (online and CATI) were integrated and validated for comparability before final tabulation and weighting.
- Cleaning, imputation, etc: Imputation and linkage were primarily used for income-related variables. Personal and family income data were linked to administrative tax records for approximately 82% of respondents. For remaining cases with missing linkage or incomplete information, donor imputation was applied to fill gaps using records from similar respondents. Since income questions were not directly asked in 2018, this administrative linkage fully replaced self-reported values. Minor imputations and derived variable calculations (e.g., total giving, volunteering hours) were also conducted to ensure consistency and completeness.
- Sources of error: 
    - Sampling error: Because the survey used a probability sample rather than a census, all estimates are subject to sampling variability.
    - Coverage error: The sampling frame relied on telephone numbers linked to addresses and the dwelling frame. Some groups‚Äîsuch as individuals without stable housing, people in institutions, and residents in the three territories‚Äîwere excluded, which may lead to undercoverage.
    - Non-response error: Differences between respondents and non-respondents (e.g., people less engaged in volunteering may be less likely to participate) can bias results.
    - Measurement error: Respondents may misreport their giving or volunteering activities due to recall bias or social desirability, particularly when estimating hours or amounts donated.
    - Processing error: Although edits and validation were applied, data entry and coding errors may still occur.
- Limitations, known biases, etc:
    - The introduction of an online mode for the first time in 2018 may have affected comparability with previous cycles.
    - The ‚Äúrejective sampling‚Äù design, where all volunteers received the long questionnaire while non-volunteers were subsampled, can complicate weighting and introduce selection bias if not properly adjusted.
    - The target population excludes residents of Yukon, Northwest Territories, Nunavut, and institutional populations, limiting national representativeness.
    - Administrative linkage for income variables improved accuracy but created possible differences compared with self-reported data from prior cycles.
    - Mode and questionnaire changes mean that direct comparisons with earlier GSS-GVP cycles are not recommended.
    - Potential non-response bias remains, especially among highly mobile or disadvantaged populations.
    - Undercoverage bias may persist for households without telephone access or those difficult to reach via online or phone modes.
