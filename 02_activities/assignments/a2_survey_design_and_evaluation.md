### Part B - Survey Evaluation:

For the **Canadian General Social Survey on Giving, Volunteering, and Participating, 2018 (cycle 33)**, conducted by Statistics Canada find any and all available documentation for the data gathered and identify and describe the survey features indicated below.

1. Sample type
The sample was drawn from the Census data per household, and then stratified by province and population group. In each of the strata, a systematic sampling method was used to ensure representativeness.

2. Sample size
The total sample size was 80,000 individuals. These were composed of 60,000 regular sample and 20,000 oversample (added to enhance the accuracy of estimates for specific subgroups)

3. Target population
The target population for the 2023 SGVP is all persons 15 years of age and older in Canada. 
This excluded: 
- Residents of the Yukon, Northwest Territories, and Nunavut
- Full-time residents of institutions
- Residents of First Nations reserves

4. Sampling frame
The sampling frame was based on the 2021 Census of Population, but supplemented with additional administrative data to improve coverage.

5. Survey mode(s) 
Data was collected using a mixed-modes approach:
1) telephone interviews
2) Self-completed electronic questionnaires

6. Timeline
The data collection period started on Sept 15, 2023, and completed on Mar 30, 2024.

7. Response rate
The overall response rate was 40.9% (42.2% for the regular sample and 37.1% for the oversample)

8. Weights
The survey estimates were adjusted by weighting to ensure representativeness of the target population. These weights helped account for:
- stratificiation
- non-response

9. Data processing
Data validation: data quality checks throughout the data collection period (e.x., "checking the respondent's reported age against the date of birth coming from the sample file").
Data editing: reviewing and fixing inconsitent or missing values.
Weighting: applying different weights to adjust for factors such as non-response.

10. Cleaning, imputation, etc
Imputation was used for the following variables:
1) personal income
2) family income
3) variables related to donations
4) formal volunteering variables
5) informal volunteering variables.

11. Sources of error
The report lists the following potential sources of error:
- sampling error: possibility of the sample not beinf a perfect representation of the population
- non-sampling error: due to non-response bias (individuals who did not respond to the survey or declined) and/or measurement error (issues with data collection or participants not understanding the questions)

12. Limitations, known biases, etc
Some indicated limitations and biases are:
- non-response bias (some groups may not have been accurately represented)
- mode (differences in survey resposne based on whether the interview was over the phone or by electronic questionnaire)

Respondents will be contacted using publicly available voter information or local directories. The questionnaire will be administered in a mixed-mode method of telephone and electronic survey. 

If respondents meet any of the following criteria, their responses will not be included and another respondent will not be selected: decline to participate, are not eligible to vote, or have decided not to vote in this election. 

As the populations in each of these ridings are very large, taking a sample of approximately 150 completed surveys per riding gives us enough data to identify some key trends. This smaller sample size is appropriate for a short political opinion survey where the goal is to capture key themes instead of details and/or precise responses.
